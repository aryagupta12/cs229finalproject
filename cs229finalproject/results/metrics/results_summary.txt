2. UNCERTAINTY ANALYSIS — MLP + MC Dropout (50 forward passes)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Uncertainty-Error Correlation : 0.1658
  (positive = higher uncertainty → more prediction errors, as expected)

Error rate by uncertainty level (split at median variance):
  Low uncertainty  (confident half)  : 21.3% error rate
  High uncertainty (uncertain half)  : 35.1% error rate
  → Uncertain predictions are 65% more likely to be wrong

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4. KEY FINDINGS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[1] Neural networks do NOT outperform logistic regression on this task.
    MLP AUC (0.790) ≈ Logistic Regression AUC (0.788). The structured,
    aggregated nature of the features limits the benefit of deep models.

[2] MC Dropout uncertainty estimates are meaningful and actionable.
    The variance across 50 stochastic forward passes correctly identifies
    harder cases: error rate rises from 21% → 35% as uncertainty increases.

[3] Selective prediction is the clearest practical benefit.
    A decision-maker using this model could abstain on uncertain cases
    and achieve AUC 0.862 on the cases they do act on (vs. 0.790 overall).

[4] LSTM underperforms significantly (AUC 0.753, Brier 0.476).
    This confirms that the sequential architecture adds no value when
    features are time-aggregated rather than genuinely time-varying.

[5] Logistic Regression is the best-calibrated model (ECE = 0.019).
    The MLP's ECE improves from 0.069 → 0.064 with MC Dropout averaging,
    but neither matches the logistic regression's calibration.
